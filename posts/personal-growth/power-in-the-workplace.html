Early in my working life I assumed power at work came from your title. The higher the box on the org chart, the more power you had. It took me embarrassingly long to notice how many people with modest titles ran the place, and how many senior people didn't.

Understanding the real sources of power made work far less stressful — not because I wanted to grab it, but because I could finally see what was actually going on.

Positional power is the weakest kind

The authority that comes with a title is real but shallow. It evaporates the moment you change jobs, and people follow it grudgingly at best. If it's the only power you have, you're more fragile than you look.

The durable kinds

The power that travels with you comes from other places: expertise (you're the person who actually knows), relationships (you're trusted and well-connected), information (you see what others don't), and reputation (people already believe in you before you walk in). None of these are handed out. All of them compound.

Why this makes work calmer

Once you can name the currents, office politics stops feeling like weather that happens to you. You stop resenting the game and start playing your own version of it — quietly building expertise, relationships, and reputation, which are worth having regardless of where you work next.

Power isn't a dirty word. It's just the ability to get good things done. The only real question is which kind you're building — the fragile kind that lives in a title, or the durable kind that lives in you.
