I used to treat relationships as a binary: someone was either a friend or they weren't. That framing made me clumsy — too much too soon with some people, too little too late with others. What helped was picturing every relationship as sitting on a rung of a ladder.

The rungs

Roughly, they go: stranger, acquaintance, friend, trusted ally. Everyone you know is on one of these rungs, and the whole point is that you climb them one step at a time. You don't leap from stranger to ally, however much you'd like to.

You climb through small deposits

Movement up the ladder happens through repeated, low-stakes generosity — a useful intro, remembering a detail, showing up when it's mildly inconvenient. Each one is a small deposit of trust. Skipping rungs, by asking for too much before you've made those deposits, is the single most common way people stall a relationship.

Match your ask to the rung

The practical payoff: calibrate what you ask for to where the relationship actually is. A big favour from an acquaintance feels like an imposition; the same favour from a trusted ally feels natural. Know the rung, and you'll rarely misjudge the ask.

It sounds transactional written down. In practice it's the opposite — it's a reminder to be patient with people, to invest before you need anything, and to let closeness build at the pace trust actually moves.
