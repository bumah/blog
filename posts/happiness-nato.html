The more I think and reflect, the more I've come to believe that happiness is really a choice. A lot of our unhappiness comes down to a simple gap: the distance between what we desire and what we actually have. When the two line up, we feel content. When they don't — when the thing we wanted doesn't arrive, or the outcome we expected doesn't land — we feel let down.

Follow that logic and something revealing comes out of it. The more desires you carry, the more expectations you set, the more chances you create to feel disappointed. Every want is a small bet, and every bet can lose. The person with a hundred burning desires has given themselves a hundred ways to feel let down. The person who wants almost nothing has almost nothing to lose.

So how do you close that gap? I think there are only two doors.

Door one: desire less

The first path is to reduce your desires.

This is the way of the monk. Strip life back to the essentials — food, water, shelter, the people you love — and want for little beyond them. If your desires are few and simple, they're easy to meet, and once they're met, you're content. There's very little gap left between what you want and what you have, so there's very little room to be unhappy.

I have real respect for this path. There's a deep wisdom in it, and anyone who's ever felt the relief of wanting less knows it isn't nonsense. A simpler life is often a calmer, happier one.

But I'll be honest: it isn't the path I chose. I still want things. I want to build, to achieve, to chase ideas, to see where my ambition leads. Asking me to switch that off wouldn't make me peaceful — it would make me restless in a different way. For people wired like me, "just want less" isn't really available.

So I needed a second door.

Door two: desire freely, but manage how you desire

The second path keeps the desires — all of them — and instead gets smarter about how you hold them.

If you can't desire less, then the goal becomes managing desire well. Over the years I've leaned on three tools to do exactly that. The first two set you up so you're let down less often. The third is what catches you when you're let down anyway — because you will be. Together, they're how I try to hold on to my happiness rather than leave it to chance.

Tool one: diversify

Don't put all your eggs in one basket — not with your money, and not with your happiness.

If your entire sense of wellbeing rests on a single desire — one relationship, one job, one goal — you've made a dangerous bet. Lose that one thing and you don't just lose the thing; you lose the whole foundation you were standing on. That's how people fall into real despair. Not because one thing failed, but because that one thing was quietly carrying everything.

Spread your desires across a few areas of life — your work, your health, your relationships, the things you do purely for joy — and no single loss can flatten you. When one falls short, the others hold you up while you recover. It's the same logic I'd apply to managing any risk: never let one exposure be big enough to sink you.

Tool two: control

Be careful not to hang your strongest desires on things you can't control — because the moment you do, your happiness is sitting in someone else's hands.

A promotion your boss decides. A result a market determines. Another person's feelings. You're allowed to want those things, but notice how little of the outcome is actually yours. Tie your deepest happiness to them and you've handed the keys to someone, or something, outside yourself.

Where you have the choice, anchor your biggest desires to what you can actually affect. And where you can't control the outcome, shift your focus to the part you can: the effort, the process, the journey. If you measure yourself by how well you showed up rather than whether the result landed, you'll rarely be disappointed — because that part was always yours to win.

Tool three: NATO — Not Attached To Outcome

Here's the thing about the first two tools. They stack the deck in your favour, but they don't guarantee anything. You can diversify well, focus on what you control, and still get let down. Life will still surprise you. The result you worked for still won't always come.

That's where the last and most important tool comes in. NATO. Not Attached To Outcome.

The principle is simple. You're allowed to want things, to want them badly, to work hard for them. What you let go of is the outcome — the demand that things turn out exactly the way you pictured. You pour yourself into the effort, then release your grip on the result.

It sounds like a small distinction. It changes everything. Because most of our suffering doesn't come from wanting things or working for them — that part is often energising. The suffering comes from the attachment: the clenched insistence that a specific outcome must happen, and the crash when it doesn't.

And when something does fall through, NATO is what gets you back on your feet — through what I'd call radical acceptance. That simply means meeting reality as it is, rather than as you wanted it to be. Not pretending the disappointment doesn't sting. Just a clear-eyed acknowledgement: this is what happened, and fighting the fact of it only adds a second layer of suffering on top of the first. The job fell through. The relationship ended. The venture didn't work. You can spend months at war with that reality, or you can accept that it went the way it went and put your energy into what comes next. The event already cost you something. Refusing to accept it just makes you pay twice.

That's why NATO is the one that matters most. Diversification and control reduce how often you're let down. NATO makes sure that when you are, you're okay — and that you recover quickly.

What this looks like in a real life

This isn't abstract for me. I try to use all three everywhere.

The strange thing is that holding outcomes loosely hasn't made me less ambitious or caring. If anything, it's made me bolder. When you're not terrified of a bad result, you're free to actually try — to reach for things you'd never risk if your whole happiness depended on them working out.

The choice, in the end

So when I say happiness is a choice, this is what I mean. It's easy to attain — and just as easy to lose.

You can desire less, like the monk, and find peace in simplicity. Or you can desire freely, and hold it all with an open hand. I chose the second door. And if you choose the second door too, the secret, I believe, is to be intentional — be careful about what you want, focus more on what you control, and be at peace with the outcomes.

The bottom line: it's okay to wish for things and work for them. But always enjoy the journey — and if the result isn't what you hoped, accept it, and keep walking.
