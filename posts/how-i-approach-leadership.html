I didn't start out good at leading people. I'll be honest about that, because it's the whole reason I ended up needing a model in the first place. And I'm still learning how to be a better leader every day.

When I first started managing people — and later, mentoring — I made most of the mistakes you can make. I talked more than I listened. I handed out instructions and assumed they'd landed. I got frustrated when things didn't go my way. And when projects failed, when startups and products didn't work, my first instinct was to look around for who was responsible. Usually anyone but me.

It took me longer than I'd like to admit to see the pattern: the common thread in all of it was me. Not the team, not bad luck. Me — how I was showing up, or failing to.

So I started paying attention. I watched the leaders I actually respected and tried to work out what they were doing that I wasn't. I wanted something simple — something I could reach for in any moment, with my team, my colleagues, my peers, even my family and myself. That's how I came up with the LEAD framework.

What is LEAD? LEAD stands for: Listen. Emotional intelligence. Accountability. Direction. Four key traits I believe every great leader should possess.

L is for Listen

This is the one I got most wrong for the longest.

In almost every interaction, I was talking. Giving instructions, setting expectations, moving on to the next thing. I thought that was what leading looked like — being the one with the answers, the one directing traffic. What I completely missed was that talking isn't leading. Most of the time, it's just noise.

When I finally started listening — really listening, not just waiting for my turn to speak — the difference was immediate. People told me things I'd been too busy to hear. Problems surfaced earlier. I understood what was actually going on rather than what I assumed was going on. It turns out the person doing the work usually knows more about it than the person assigning it. I'd just never given them the space to tell me.

Listening first still doesn't come naturally to me. But I've learned that the quality of everything downstream — the decisions, the trust, the results — depends on it.

E is for Emotional intelligence

The second thing I had to face was how I reacted when things went wrong.

I used to get upset when things didn't go my way. Visibly. And when you're leading people, your reaction sets the temperature for everyone around you. If you lose your composure the moment something slips, you teach your team two things: that setbacks are catastrophes, and that they'd better hide problems from you rather than bring them to you. Neither helps anyone.

Emotional intelligence, for me, has two sides. The first is managing my own reactions — pausing before I respond, remembering that most problems are smaller than they feel in the moment. The second is reading other people: noticing when someone's struggling, when the room has gone quiet for a reason, when what someone says isn't quite what they mean. I'm still working on both. But even getting a little better at it changed how people felt around me — and how honest they were willing to be.

A is for Accountability

This is the one that changed me the most, because it is often the hardest to swallow.

When projects failed, my instinct was to find the reason outside myself. The colleague who didn't deliver. The team that dropped the ball. It's a comfortable place to stand, blame.

The shift came when I finally asked a different question: not "who let this happen?" but "what was my part in this?" And the answer, every time, was: more than I wanted to admit. I'd set unclear expectations. I hadn't listened. I hadn't given people what they needed to succeed. Once I started owning that — genuinely, not as a performance — everything changed. Because the moment a failure becomes yours, so does the power to fix it. Blame is passive. Accountability is where the control lives.

The best leaders I've watched do this instinctively. When things go well, they point to the team. When things go badly, they point to themselves. I try to do the same. I don't always manage it, but I catch myself faster now.

D is for Direction

The last one sounds obvious, but many get it wrong constantly.

I would pass on a task or a deliverable and assume the person understood exactly what I meant. Then I'd be frustrated when what came back wasn't what I'd pictured. For a long time I blamed them for that too — until I realised the fault was mine. The picture was clear in my head. I'd just never actually made it clear to them.

Direction is the leader's job that no one else can do: making sure people know where you're going and what "good" looks like. It's not about micromanaging every step. It's about clarity — being explicit about the destination so people aren't left guessing, and can then use their own judgment to get there. Vague direction isn't freedom for your team. It's a trap you've set for them without meaning to.

Now I try to slow down at the start of things. Spell out what I'm actually after. Check it landed. It feels slower in the moment, and it saves an enormous amount of frustration later — for everyone.

Leading myself first

The thing I didn't expect is how much LEAD applies beyond managing a team. With myself, am I listening to what my own life is telling me? Am I managing my reactions? Am I holding myself accountable, or looking for something to blame? Am I clear about the direction I'm actually heading?

I see the LEAD system more like a checklist to run when an interaction matters — a quick way to catch yourself before falling back into the old habits of talking too much, reacting too fast, blaming too easily, or assuming you've been clearer than you have.

I'm still learning to be a better leader who listens. LEAD is just how I keep myself on track — with my team, and with everyone else who matters.
