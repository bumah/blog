"What's the meaning of life?" might be the most asked question in human history. Millions of people have sat with it. I've asked it myself, and over the years I've put it to friends too — and what strikes me is that almost everyone has an answer, but no two are quite the same. I'm sure you have your own. That's the point, really: the meaning isn't one fixed thing handed down to all of us. It's personal.

For me, the reason the question mattered at all came down to something simpler underneath it: I wanted to feel fulfilled. I wanted to be happy, in the deeper sense of the word — not just good days, but a life that felt like it added up to something.

I'm a systems guy. I'm always looking for simple ways to remember things and actually put them into practice, because I believe the easier it is to remember a framework, the easier it is to implement. So after a fair bit of soul searching, I landed on four simple pillars that spelled out the word itself. LIFE.

Legacy. Interest. Future. Entertainment.

I'm not going to pretend this is the secret of the universe. It's just the most useful way I've found to think about how a life actually holds together — and, for me, where fulfilment actually comes from. Let me walk you through each one, because the meaning is in the parts.

L is for Legacy

Here's the thing about legacy that took me too long to understand: it isn't about you.

We tend to hear the word and picture a statue, a name on a building, something that keeps us alive after we're gone. But when I really looked at it, legacy was never pointing back at me. It was pointing at everyone else. Legacy is simply what you leave behind for others.

And "others" is personal. For some it's their kids — the education you make possible, the doors you open, the opportunities you hand them that you never had yourself. For others it stretches wider: your loved ones, your family, the community you're part of, your country, even the world if you're lucky enough to move something at that scale.

There's no correct size. A parent who gives their child a steadier start than they had is building a legacy every bit as real as someone funding a hospital wing. What matters is the direction of it — outward, toward people who aren't you. The day I stopped asking "what will I be remembered for" and started asking "what am I leaving for them," the whole idea got lighter and more honest. And, strangely, more fulfilling — because building something for others turns out to be one of the most satisfying things a person can do.

I is for Interest

If legacy is about others, interest is about you — no apology needed.

These are the things you do that make you come alive. Not because they're useful, or because they pay, or because they impress anyone. Just the things that switch something on in you and pull you fully into the moment.

For one person it's the piano. For another it's bird watching, or basketball, or travel, or gardening. The specific thing matters far less than the feeling it gives you, which is the feeling of being fully present in your own life.

I used to treat my interests as the thing I'd get to after the important stuff was handled — a reward for a productive week. I had it backwards. They're not the leftover. They're a big part of what all the rest is for. A life where the obligations are met but nothing lights you up isn't really working. It's just being maintained. And being maintained is not the same as being happy.

F is for Future

Then there's the unglamorous one. Future is about securing the conditions that let you do everything else — and it comes down to two things: your health and your wealth.

Health is the one we postpone until it forces our hand. But it's the platform under all of it. Your legacy, your interests, your ability to laugh and dance and travel — every one of them assumes a body that can show up. Looking after your health isn't vanity and it isn't a separate hobby. It's the thing that keeps the door open to all the others.

Wealth is the same logic pointed at resources. Not money for its own sake — money as the means to actually do the things that matter to you. To make the legacy possible. To afford the time your interests need. You don't have to be rich. You do have to be honest that the future you want has a cost, and that quietly preparing for it is part of the work.

Future is the least exciting letter, and the one most people get wrong simply by ignoring it. It's the quiet work that makes everything else possible — and, just as importantly, it's what removes the low background hum of anxiety that quietly eats away at happiness when you know you're not looking after tomorrow.

E is for Entertainment

And finally, the one I almost left off — and I'm glad I didn't, because leaving it off would have said a lot.

Entertainment, to me, is about being around people or in environments that bring so much positive energy and fun that you can lose yourself in the moment and experience the world for what it is. Comedy. Dancing. A table full of people you love, all talking at once. The gatherings you walk away from lighter than you arrived.

I think most of us already know how laughing makes us feel — and still don't prioritise it enough. We let life take over and the days just fly by. But think about how you feel after a real laugh, the kind that catches you off guard. Something resets. Whatever you were carrying gets put down for a second, and you remember that the point was never just to grind toward some finish line. Part of it is simply to enjoy being here.

Entertainment is what keeps the other three from turning into a chore. Legacy can get heavy. Future can get anxious. Even interests can start to feel like work. Laughter keeps it human. It's the most immediate happiness of the four — the one you feel in the moment rather than build over years.

Putting the four together

What I like about LIFE — the reason it stuck when the big abstract question never did — is that the four letters keep each other honest.

Legacy without entertainment turns into duty with no joy in it. Interest without a secured future is something you eventually can't afford. Future without legacy is just looking after yourself with no real point beyond it. And entertainment without the other three feels good in the moment and empty soon after.

This is the part I didn't expect: fulfilment and happiness were never about getting one of these right. They came from keeping all four alive at once. When I feel off — restless, flat, like something's missing — I can almost always trace it to one of these four going quiet. Too much grinding for the future, no time for interests. Plenty of fun, nothing being built for anyone else. The framework doesn't tell me what to do so much as it tells me which letter I've been neglecting. And nine times out of ten, the letter I've been neglecting is exactly the reason I haven't felt like myself.

I don't think there's one meaning of life waiting to be discovered. I think there are four threads, and both the meaning and the happiness are in keeping all of them alive at once.

That's what LIFE means to me. I'd be curious what it spells for you.
