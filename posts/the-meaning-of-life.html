"What's the meaning of life?" might be the most asked question there is. I've asked it myself plenty, and over the years I've put it to friends too — and what strikes me is that almost everyone has an answer, but no two are quite the same. I'm sure you've got your own. That's kind of the point: the meaning isn't one fixed thing handed to all of us. It's personal.

For me it was never really about the grand cosmic answer. Underneath the big question was a simpler one: I just wanted to feel fulfilled. To be happy in the deeper sense — not just good days here and there, but a life that felt like it actually added up to something.

I like simple things I can actually remember and use — if a framework is easy to hold in your head, you're far more likely to live by it. So over time I landed on four things that, for me, make a life feel full. And they happen to spell the word itself. LIFE.

Love. Ikigai. Fun. Explore.

I'm not claiming this is the secret of the universe. It's just the most honest way I've found to think about where my own fulfilment comes from. Let me walk through each one.

L is for Love — starting with yourself

For a long time I thought love was all about pouring into other people — family, friends, the people you're responsible for. And that matters, hugely. But I had the order wrong. It starts with loving yourself first.

Not in a vain, self-obsessed way. I mean actually being kind to yourself: forgiving your own mistakes, talking to yourself the way you'd talk to a good friend, looking after your body and your mind, not tying your entire worth to what you achieve. For years I was my own harshest critic, and it quietly poisoned everything else.

Here's what I've come to believe: you can't really pour into anyone else from an empty cup. When I'm at peace with myself, I've got so much more to give the people around me. Loving yourself isn't the selfish option — it's the thing that makes all the other love possible.

I is for Ikigai

Ikigai is a Japanese idea that roughly means "a reason for being" — the thing that gets you out of bed in the morning. I love it because it sits right at the crossroads of what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, and what you can be rewarded for.

For me that's the work — the building, the ideas, the stuff I'd probably do even if no one paid me, but that also happens to be useful to other people. It's the closest thing I have to a sense of purpose, and I've learned that without it I drift. A life of pure comfort with nothing to get up for sounds lovely for about a week, and then something in me starts to go stale.

I don't think everyone's ikigai has to be their job. It can be raising your kids, a craft, a cause, a community you serve. What matters is having something bigger than yourself that pulls you forward.

F is for Fun

This is the one I used to treat as a reward for getting the serious stuff done — and I had it completely backwards.

Fun isn't the leftover. It's a big part of the point. The laughing-till-it-hurts dinners, the dancing, the silly moments with people you love, the days you walk away from lighter than you arrived. That stuff isn't a distraction from a meaningful life — for me it's a huge part of what makes it meaningful in the first place.

I notice it most when it's missing. When I've gone weeks heads-down and serious, something goes flat, even if everything's technically "going well." A proper laugh resets me. It reminds me that the point was never just to grind toward some finish line — part of it is simply to enjoy being here.

E is for Explore

The last one is about staying curious — refusing to let life shrink into the same loop on repeat.

Explore, for me, is new places, new ideas, new people, new skills. Travel is the obvious one, and I love it, but it's bigger than that. It's picking up a book on something I know nothing about, saying yes to the thing just outside my comfort zone, keeping the door open to being surprised.

I think a life stops feeling alive the moment you stop exploring. The routine takes over, the years blur, and one day you look up and realise nothing new has happened in a long while. Exploring is how I keep life feeling wide rather than narrow — how I make sure I'm still growing instead of just repeating.

Putting the four together

What I like about LIFE — the reason it stuck — is that the four keep each other honest.

Love without ikigai can turn into comfort with no purpose. Ikigai without fun becomes a grind. Fun without exploring gets shallow fast. And exploring without loving yourself first is just running — chasing the next thing to avoid sitting still with who you are.

Here's what I didn't expect: fulfilment was never about nailing one of these. It came from keeping all four alive at once. When I feel off — restless, flat, like something's missing — I can nearly always trace it back to one of them going quiet. Too much grind, no fun. Plenty of comfort, no purpose. The framework doesn't tell me what to do so much as show me which letter I've been neglecting. And nine times out of ten, that's exactly why I haven't felt like myself.

I don't think there's one meaning of life waiting to be discovered. I think there are a few threads, and the meaning — and the happiness — is in keeping all of them alive at once.

That's what LIFE means to me right now. I'd be curious what it spells for you.
