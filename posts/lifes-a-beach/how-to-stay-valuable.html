The world I'm working in reshuffles fast enough that betting on any single job, tool, or skill lasting forever seems naive. So instead of chasing security in a role, I've tried to build it in myself — to stay valuable regardless of which way the ground shifts.

Solve problems, don't just do tasks

Tasks get automated and outsourced; the ability to figure out what should be done doesn't. The more your value lives in judgement — knowing which problem matters and how to approach it — the harder you are to replace. Aim to be the person who defines the work, not only the one who executes it.

Keep learning in public

Skills decay. The habit of learning doesn't. I try to always be visibly getting better at something — sharing what I learn as I go, which compounds twice: once in the skill, once in the reputation for having it.

Build rare combinations

You don't have to be the best in the world at one thing. Being unusually good at two or three things that rarely go together makes you close to unique. The combination is the moat — anyone can copy a single skill, but the intersection is yours.

Stay useful to people, not just to systems

Relationships outlast roles. The people who know you're reliable and good will carry you between jobs, industries, even careers. Staying valuable is as much about the trust you've banked as the skills you've stacked.

None of this is about hustling harder. It's about quietly making yourself the kind of person who has options — which, not coincidentally, is also the kind of person who worries less.
