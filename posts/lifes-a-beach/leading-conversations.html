For a long time I thought the person leading a conversation was the one doing most of the talking. It's almost exactly backwards. The people who quietly run a room are usually the ones asking the questions, not answering them.

Leading a conversation, the way I've come to think about it, is about steering — gently pointing the exchange somewhere useful and making the other person feel genuinely heard on the way.

Questions are the steering wheel

Whoever asks the questions sets the direction. A good open question — "what made you get into that?" — does more work than any clever thing you could say about yourself. It hands the other person the floor and, paradoxically, puts you in control of where things go.

Listen to understand, not to reply

Most of us listen with a loaded response, just waiting for our turn. People feel that. The shift that changed things for me was listening only to understand — and letting my next line come from what they actually said, not from what I'd pre-loaded.

Leave them better than you found them

The simplest test of a conversation well led: does the other person walk away feeling a little more interesting, a little more understood, than before? Do that consistently and people seek you out. It costs nothing but attention.

I'm still bad at this more often than I'd like. But treating conversation as something you steer rather than something that happens to you has quietly changed how most of my days go.
