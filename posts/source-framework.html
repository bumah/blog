I've worked on a lot of ideas. Some my own, plenty helping other people with theirs. And when I wasn't building, I was watching — following companies, tracking who broke out and who quietly disappeared. Over time you start to notice things.

When I was younger, the thrill of building something new was reason enough. The idea itself was the reward. I didn't think too hard about whether it would actually work, because the fun was in the making.

That changes as you get older. You take on responsibilities. Your time stops feeling infinite. And you start to care a great deal about not pouring months of your life into something that was never going to work in the first place. I still love building — I just can't afford to build the wrong things anymore.

So I did what my instincts as a risk person told me to do. I worked the problem backwards. Instead of asking "why might this succeed?" — which is easy to answer, because you can always dream up a reason — I asked the harder question: "why do startups fail?" I reverse-engineered the failures, looked for the patterns underneath them, and tried to turn those patterns into a filter I could run on any idea before committing my time.

What came out of that was six questions. They spell SOURCE.

Severity. Occurrence. Urgency. Replicability. Competition. Endurance.

None of them is complicated on its own. The power is in running all six together, honestly, before you fall in love with an idea — because the failures I studied almost always looked great until you asked one of these questions and the answer was uncomfortable.

S — Severity: how much does the problem actually hurt?

The first question is the most important one, and the one people skip because they don't want to hear the answer. Is this a problem that genuinely hurts people, or one that merely annoys them?

People tolerate annoyances forever. They pay to make pain go away. A payment failure blocking someone's revenue, being unable to work, being locked out of credit, a health scare — those hurt, and people move quickly to solve them. "It'd be nice if the interface were cleaner" does not hurt. It's a preference, and preferences don't open wallets.

If the pain is real, people will pay. If it's mild, they'll procrastinate forever — and you'll spend your life trying to convince them to care.

O — Occurrence: how often does it happen?

A painful problem that strikes once a decade is a very different business from one that strikes every day.

Frequent problems mean a larger market and repeated chances to serve the same customer. Rare ones — buying a house, moving, getting married — mean a smaller market and a long, cold gap between transactions. That doesn't automatically kill an idea; a rare problem can still be a great business if it's severe enough and the transaction is valuable enough. But you need to know which game you're playing before you start.

U — Urgency: how fast do people need it solved?

Severity is about how much it hurts. Urgency is about how quickly people act.

Some problems demand a solution right now — the system is down, the cash is needed today, it's an emergency. People buy those instantly and pay a premium. Other problems are real but easy to postpone: getting fit, learning a skill, sorting out your finances. People agree they matter and then do nothing for six months. Low urgency isn't fatal, but it means you'll be in the business of building habits and fighting procrastination rather than simply taking orders.

R — Replicability: does it scale without being rebuilt?

Can you solve this once and reuse the exact same solution everywhere — or does every new market force you to start again?

Software that works identically in every country scales toward enormous outcomes. A model tangled up in local regulation, culture, or logistics — real estate, banking, hyperlocal services — caps out at regional scale unless you rebuild it each time. Low replicability doesn't mean don't do it. It means the business is a different shape: a geographic-dominance play or a roll-up, not a global rocket. Just know that going in.

C — Competition: what stops someone copying you?

This is the one founders are most tempted to wave away, and it's often the one that kills them.

If your only edge is "slightly better" or "a bit cheaper," you don't have an edge — you have a head start that any funded competitor will erase. A real advantage is something hard to copy: network effects, switching costs, a data lead that compounds, a regulatory or distribution position others can't easily match. Without one, you're heading straight into a race to the bottom, and the incumbents will win it. Be honest here. A me-too product with no moat is the most common good-looking idea that quietly fails.

E — Endurance: will this problem still exist in five years?

Finally, is this a permanent problem, or are you riding a trend that's about to fade?

Some problems only grow — digital payments, remote work, AI. Others are here today and gone in a few years, tied to a moment or a fad. Building on an enduring problem gives you time to compound. Building on a temporary one means you're in a race against the clock, and you'd better know your exit before the window closes. Trend-riding can make money, but it's a fundamentally different bet from building something lasting — and if you're trying to spend your time wisely, that distinction matters.

How I actually use it

I don't treat SOURCE as a precise score. I treat it as six honest questions, and I look at the overall picture they paint.

Roughly, if five or six come back strong, that's an exceptional opportunity — rare, and worth clearing the decks for. Four strong is a genuinely good one. Three is viable but constrained, and you'd better love it. Two or fewer, and I've usually just saved myself six months.

Two of the six carry extra weight in my experience. A weak Severity or a weak Competitive advantage is often fatal on its own, no matter how well the others score — because a problem that doesn't hurt and a business anyone can copy are two of the surest ways to end up with nothing. If either of those is low, I need a very good reason to keep going.

But here's the part I find most useful, and it's why the framework earns its place rather than just talking me out of things. A low pillar doesn't only warn you — it tells you what kind of business you'd have to build. Low urgency? Then everything depends on habit and retention, so build for that or don't bother. Low occurrence? Then forget growth metrics and make each transaction count. Low replicability? Then win one geography completely instead of spreading thin. The weak spot isn't just a red flag; it's a map of what you'd be signing up for.

What a low score is really telling you

Since that last point is the one I lean on most, here's how I read each pillar when it comes back weak — the impact it creates, and the play worth considering if you still want to go ahead.

Low Severity — the problem annoys, it doesn't hurt. Impact: people won't pay much, margins are thin, and they forget you exist. To make it work, you're no longer selling a cure — you're building a habit. Go for daily engagement, freemium or ad-supported models, and the kind of scale where a tiny paid conversion still adds up. Or dig until you find an adjacent problem that actually hurts, and pivot toward that.

Low Occurrence — the need is real but rare. Impact: a small market, slow growth, and expensive customers you reach once and rarely again. To make it work, stop chasing usage metrics and maximise the value of each transaction instead. Own the whole journey around that rare event, target customers who can afford premium pricing, and win the search results so you're there the moment they finally need you.

Low Urgency — real, but easy to postpone. Impact: low conversion, long sales cycles, people who agree it matters and then do nothing. To make it work, obsess over retention above everything. Build the habit before you try to monetise it — streaks, community, accountability, small wins — and use commitment devices like subscriptions to keep people engaged long enough to stick.

Low Replicability — it won't scale without being rebuilt. Impact: every new market means starting again, a ceiling on how big you can get, and no network effects across regions. To make it work, stop thinking globally and dominate one geography completely. Build local moats — relationships, regulation, data — add services within your market rather than expanding outward, and accept that a great regional business is still a great business. Or build once and license the model elsewhere.

Low Competitive advantage — anyone can copy you. Impact: price wars, no switching costs, collapsing margins, customers who leave for the next slightly-better option. To make it work, don't fight incumbents on their own turf. Find a dimension they've ignored, own a specific segment deeply, and manufacture a moat you didn't start with — integrations, community, data lock-in, brand. And if you're honest with yourself and there's still no real edge, that's often the signal to walk away.

Low Endurance — you're riding a trend that will fade. Impact: a short runway, a problem that may vanish before you've built anything lasting. To make it work, treat time as the scarce resource it is: grow fast, extract cash rather than reinvesting endlessly, and know your exit before you start. Better still, bolt on features that address a permanent need so you have somewhere to stand when the trend passes.

Why I bother

None of this guarantees anything. Plenty of ideas that score well still fail on execution, timing, or plain bad luck — no filter removes that. What SOURCE does is stop me falling for the ideas that were never going to work, the ones that look exciting right up until you ask the six uncomfortable questions.

When I was younger I'd have found all this a bit joyless — why analyse the magic out of building something? But that's not how I see it now. My time is the one thing I can't make more of. Running six honest questions before I commit months of it isn't killing the thrill. It's making sure that when I do throw myself into something, it's something with a real chance of working.

That, to me, is the whole point. Not to build less — to build the right things.
