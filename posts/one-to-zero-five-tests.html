Most startup analysis runs the wrong direction. We look at a company that already won, admire what it became, and reverse-engineer a tidy story about why it was obviously going to work. That's survivorship talking. The winners always look inevitable in hindsight — that's precisely what makes hindsight useless.

So I do the opposite. I start from the outcome — the company at the peak of its success, the one — and walk it all the way back to the moment it launched, the zero. One to zero. The interesting thing was never how good the company looks today. It's the distance it travelled to get there, and whether you could have seen the trip coming from the starting line.

To make that walk-back rigorous I use the same lens I use on my own ideas: SOURCE. Six questions I built by studying failures rather than wins. I've written before about using it as a filter before committing my own time. This is the other half — using it to read why real companies won or lost, and, more usefully, to see what they changed to get there.

The six lenses, quickly

SOURCE is six questions you ask about an opportunity. Three are about the problem. Three are about whether you can defend and keep a business built on it.

  • Severity — how badly does the problem hurt? A cure sells; an annoyance doesn't.
  • Occurrence — how often does it recur? Do people come back regularly, or once a decade?
  • Urgency — is there any alternative? If people can already solve it another way, the pull is weak.
  • Reach — how many people have this problem? The size of the pie.
  • Competition — do you have a differentiated, defensible edge? (I grade this with a value-curve read for are you even different, then VRIO for will the difference last.)
  • Endurance — will the trends carry this for years, or is it a fad with a closing window?

None is complicated alone. The power is in what they add up to.

The six become five

Here's the part I find most useful. Those six lenses aren't really the answer — they're the inputs. What you actually want to know about any company is five things, and each is a word that ends in -able. Is it valuable? repeatable? scalable? durable? profitable? Get all five and you have a great company. Miss one and you have a story about what went wrong.

The six lenses map cleanly onto the five:

The verdictComes fromThe question it answers
ValuableSeverity + UrgencyDoes it hurt, with no easy alternative?
RepeatableOccurrenceDo people keep coming back?
ScalableReachIs the market big enough to grow into?
DurableCompetition + EnduranceIs there a moat, and do the trends last?
ProfitableReach × CompetitionBig market, captured with a real edge?

Two things fall out of this map that changed how I read companies.

First, it's a ladder, not a checklist. Roughly in this order: be valuable, then repeatable, then scalable, then durable, then profitable. Most startups clear the first two and die on the last two — which is why so many beloved products with huge user numbers still go to zero.

Second, profitable is the hardest gate because it draws on the most. Profit is Reach times Competition — and it's multiplicative. A huge market captured with no edge still yields nothing.

Reach is easy; edge is everything

That multiplication is worth sitting with, because it explains the most common expensive failure: the company that reaches everyone and earns nothing.

Uber and Spotify both reach practically the whole planet — Reach is maxed. Yet both fought for years to make money, and the reason is the same: a weak edge. Uber has almost no switching costs; riders and drivers price-compare with Lyft in seconds. Spotify doesn't own its catalogue — the record labels do, and they license the identical songs to Apple and take the lion's share of the revenue. Enormous reach, little pricing power. Reach × a weak edge is still a weak number.

Reach gets you revenue. Only edge converts it to profit. "Big and unprofitable" is the most crowded graveyard in tech — Webvan, MoviePass, Spotify's thin decade — and every headstone reads the same: huge Reach, weak Competition.

Grade it at launch, not today

Here's the discipline that makes any of this honest: you have to score a company as it looked the day it launched — not with everything you now know it became. Grade Airbnb today and of course it's a five across the board; that tells you nothing. Grade it in 2008 and the picture is very different — and far more instructive.

Scoring at launch also surfaces a pattern hindsight hides: repeatable can stand in for valuable. Groceries are a mild pain with alternatives on every corner — low on the value gate. But you buy them constantly, so Repeatable is maxed. A frequent-but-mild problem still builds a business. It's the whole "vitamin that becomes a habit" category: coffee, social feeds, groceries. Not urgent — just relentless.

The real trick: two scorecards

Once you commit to scoring at launch, the method reveals its best move. You don't take one snapshot — you take two. SOURCE at inception. SOURCE at success. And the whole story is the distance between them: what changed, and how the founders changed it.

Take Airbnb.

LensInception (2008)Success (~2016)What moved it
Severity34—

At launch, Airbnb was air mattresses on strangers' floors during sold-out conferences. Reach looked like a niche, Repeatable was occasional, and trusting a stranger's home was an unproven idea. That's why nearly every investor passed — and, graded honestly at zero, they weren't wrong to be cautious.

Three arrows carried it to one, and each was a deliberate move:

  • Reach 2 → 5: they widened the wedge. "Air mattresses at conferences" became "any space, any traveller, anywhere." Airbeds → whole homes → Experiences. They didn't find a big market; they redefined a small one into a big one.
  • Endurance 2 → 5: they didn't wait for the trend — they built it. Trusting strangers wasn't normal in 2008. Reviews, verified IDs, secure payments, the professional-photography programme — that infrastructure manufactured the trust the whole model depended on.
  • Competition 3 → 5: they compounded density into a moat. Scrappy early growth seeded enough supply that more guests brought more hosts brought more guests — a two-sided network effect plus a brand nobody could copy.

And notice the letter that didn't move. Occurrence stayed low — Airbnb is still a periodic-travel product. That flat arrow is exactly why, to this day, they keep pushing loyalty programmes and long stays: they're still trying to raise the one letter they never fixed. The unmoved letter predicts the company's present-day struggle. That's the framework earning its keep on a living company.

Playing the lens: how to stack the odds

This is where SOURCE stops being a scorecard and becomes a design tool. A low letter at launch isn't a death sentence — it's a to-do list. There are only two ways to win: pick an opportunity that already scores high, or take a weak one and deliberately raise it. If you're the one choosing what to build, here's how to lean on each lens.

  • Severity — go where it hurts most. Aim at the problems tied to money, time, fear, health, status. People pay to make real pain stop; they procrastinate forever on mild annoyances. Pick a hurt, not a preference.
  • Occurrence — pick frequent jobs, or narrow until they are. Favour problems people face often. And if a job is rare for the broad market, you can often raise its frequency by narrowing your segment — the same task that happens monthly for everyone might happen daily for one profession. Narrowing the target is a lever, not a limitation.
  • Urgency — hunt where alternatives are scarce. Look for problems people can't easily solve another way. Where alternatives are few or out of reach, the pull is strong and people act now. Where a decent workaround already exists, you're fighting inertia.
  • Reach — not make-or-break; it sets the shape. You can win big by solving a large-reach problem, or win well by owning a niche completely. A small market isn't a reason to quit — it's a signal to build the focused, high-value business that niche implies instead of chasing scale it can't support.
  • Competition — engineer the moat with a value curve. This is the lens you most control. Map how everyone in the space competes, then deliberately diverge — compete on a dimension the incumbents ignore. Divergence you can defend is your edge; a me-too curve is a race to the bottom.
  • Endurance — you can't make trends, but you can stand in them. This one is largely outside your hands. What you can do is choose problems a real trend is pushing forward rather than ones a fading trend is dragging down. Pick your tailwinds where you can.

So the analysis stops being a museum tour and becomes a playbook. Here's where they started, here's where they ended, here's the move that closed the gap — and here's the lever you can pull yourself.

Why I'm doing this in the open

I'm going to run this on a lot of companies — winners and failures, across industries and countries — because a framework is only worth anything if it survives contact with a hundred messy real cases. Some will confirm it. Some will break it, and those will teach me more than the ones that fit.

The promise is simple. Two scorecards, and the honest story of the distance between them. Not why the winners were destined to win — but what they actually changed to get from zero to one, named plainly enough that the next founder can do it on purpose.
