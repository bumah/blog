I've become a bit of a Hyrox convert. I wrote before about what my first one taught me about pushing through — but the more I train for these things, the more I've realised something else: beyond being fun and brutally competitive, Hyrox is almost perfectly designed for longevity. Whether it means to be or not.

If you've never come across it: Hyrox is a fitness race. You run 1km, do a functional workout station, run another 1km, do the next station — eight times through. The stations are things like the ski erg, sled push and pull, burpee broad jumps, rowing, farmers carry, sandbag lunges, and wall balls. So it's a solid hour or more of running mixed with heavy, full-body work. You don't get to be just a runner or just a lifter. You have to be both.

And that "both" is exactly why I think it's such good training for a long life.

Why it maps so neatly onto longevity

Here's the geeky bit that got me. When you read into what actually keeps people healthy into old age, two things come up again and again: cardiovascular fitness (a strong heart and good aerobic capacity) and muscle (strength you can keep as you age). They're two of the best predictors we have of both how long and how well you'll live.

The problem is most training picks a lane. Runners get the heart but neglect strength. Lifters get strong but gas out on a flight of stairs. Hyrox refuses to let you specialise — it forces cardio and strength into the same session. Which, almost by accident, makes it hit all four of my health pillars at once.

Healthy Heart. An hour of running intervals with your heart rate parked up high the whole time is about as much love as your cardiovascular system could ask for. This is the pillar Hyrox trains hardest.

Lasting Endurance. Eight kilometres of running broken up by non-stop stations builds real aerobic stamina — the ability to keep going long after you'd normally want to stop. That staying power is the definition of endurance.

Strong Body. The sleds, the carries, the lunges, the wall balls — these aren't isolation gym exercises, they're functional, whole-body strength. The exact kind that keeps you carrying shopping, lifting kids, and moving well decades from now.

Healthy Lifestyle. This one's sneakier. Having a race on the calendar gives me a reason to train consistently rather than in fits and starts. There's a real community around it, it's measurable, and it's genuinely fun — all the things that make a healthy habit actually stick instead of fizzling out in February.

Why I keep coming back to it

What I love is that I'm not doing "longevity training" in some clinical, joyless way. I'm training for a race I actually want to do well in — and the side effect is that I'm building the exact kind of fitness that should pay off for decades.

That's the sweet spot for me: something hard enough to keep me honest, fun enough to keep me coming back, and useful enough that it doubles as an investment in my future self. If you're looking for one thing to train that covers a lot of longevity ground at once, I don't think you'll find many better than this.
